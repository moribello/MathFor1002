Nozzle Flow

Calculating flow through a nozzle

Fog nozzles

Most fog or combination nozzles have a set flow in gallons per minute; these nozzles also count on a specific pressure to deliver that flow. The nozzle shown to the right is rated for 100 gpm at 100 psi. Thus, all a pump operator needs to know is the given rating for the fog / combination nozzles on their particular apparatus. Note that while the typical pressure required for these types of nozzles is 100 psi some are designed to operate at 75psi or lower pressures. An operator should be aware of all of the hose nozzles on their apparatus and their required pressures.

Fog nozzles for master stream devices typically also require 100psi "at the tip" to operate, but because of their size will deliver much more flow - upwards of 350 gpm.

Solid stream nozzles

Solid stream nozzles provide longer reach than fog / combination nozzles, but can't change their shape. These nozzles are essentially a pipe with a fixed diameter; the amount of water flowing through these nozzles (in gpm) depends on the size of the opening. Because these nozzles deliver much more water hand lines with smooth bore nozzles should be run at 50psi "at the tip" while master stream devices with solid bore nozzles require 80psi.

To determine how much water is flowing through a particular size of smooth bore nozzle we use the following formula:

GPM = 29.7 x d2 x √NP
  • GPM = Total flow of water from the nozzle in gallons per minute (gpm)
  • d = Diameter of the nozzle's opening, in inches (1", 1.5", etc.)
  • NP - Nozzle pressure. Remember that a hand line requires 50 psi, while master streams require 80 psi

As with the previous calculations, it's often helpful to review what you're plugging in to the formula before you actually start trying to perform these calculations; this formula, in general, can feel a little tricky because you're squaring one value (the diameter of the nozzle's opening) and getting the square root of another value (nozzle pressure).
